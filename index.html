2. Look

Tick what you see. Then write one short observation.

Write what is visible. Do not write who is to blame.

3. Ask if useful

Use this only when someone is available and willing to speak.

Questions about routine
  • Who usually looks after this area?
  • How often is it cleaned or cleared?
  • What usually makes the problem come back?
Questions about who can act
  • Who can agree a small change here?
  • Who controls tools, repairs, or waste collection?
  • Who should be contacted if the problem continues?
Questions about what is possible
  • What small change could happen this week?
  • What would need help from the department or university?
  • What would make the change easier to keep going?

4. Risk route

Show how the concern could move from a source to people, animals, water, food, or surfaces.

Simple route: source → way it moves → who or what it reaches → why it matters → possible control.
Complete the fields above to build the risk route sentence.
Examples

Waste: Open food waste could reach students through flies, hands, and surfaces.

Drain: Waste water could reach students through shoes, splashing, and nearby surfaces.

Medicine: Thrown-away antibiotics could enter general waste, soil, or drains.

5. Evidence

Add only useful images and short notes.

Avoid faces, names, private papers, and anything that could identify a person without need.

6. Classify

Choose the closest answer. Use “not sure” where needed.

7. Check thinking

Separate what was seen, heard, assumed, and still unknown.

8. Recommend and export

Choose what should happen next.

Choose the classification and recommendation to build a reason.
